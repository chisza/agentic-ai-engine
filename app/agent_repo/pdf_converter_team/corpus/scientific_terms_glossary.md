# Scientific and Academic Writing Glossary

## Research Methodology

**Abstract**: A brief summary (typically 150–300 words) at the beginning of a research paper that describes the study's purpose, methods, results, and conclusions.

**Hypothesis**: A proposed explanation for an observed phenomenon, formulated as a testable statement that can be supported or refuted through experimentation.

**Methodology**: The systematic, theoretical analysis of the methods applied to a field of study; describes how research is conducted.

**Peer Review**: The evaluation of scientific, academic, or professional work by others working in the same field, to ensure quality and validity before publication.

**Literature Review**: A comprehensive survey of published work relevant to a particular topic, identifying gaps, trends, and the state of knowledge.

**Control Group**: In an experiment, the group that does not receive the treatment being tested; used as a baseline for comparison.

**Variable**: Any characteristic, number, or quantity that can be measured or counted. An *independent variable* is manipulated by the researcher; a *dependent variable* is measured.

**Statistical Significance**: A result is statistically significant if it is unlikely to have occurred by chance, typically measured by a p-value below a threshold (commonly 0.05).

**p-value**: The probability of obtaining test results at least as extreme as the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A low p-value (< 0.05) indicates strong evidence against the null hypothesis.

## Data Analysis

**Mean**: The arithmetic average of a set of values; calculated by summing all values and dividing by the count.

**Median**: The middle value in a sorted list of numbers; less affected by outliers than the mean.

**Standard Deviation (SD)**: A measure of the amount of variation or dispersion in a set of values.

**Correlation**: A statistical measure that describes the degree to which two variables move in relation to each other. Ranges from −1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive).

**Regression Analysis**: A statistical method for estimating the relationships between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables.

**Confidence Interval (CI)**: A range of values that is likely to contain the true value of an unknown parameter with a stated level of confidence (e.g. 95% CI).

## Biology and Life Sciences

**DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)**: The molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all known organisms.

**RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)**: A molecule essential in gene expression; messenger RNA (mRNA) carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes for protein synthesis.

**Protein**: Large biomolecules consisting of amino acid chains that perform a vast array of functions within organisms, including catalysing reactions (enzymes) and structural roles.

**Cell**: The basic structural and functional unit of all known living organisms. Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus; eukaryotic cells have one.

**Genome**: The complete set of genetic material in an organism, including all its genes.

**Phenotype**: The observable physical or biochemical characteristics of an organism, as determined by both genetic makeup and environmental influences.

**Metabolism**: The set of life-sustaining chemical reactions in organisms that convert food to energy, build and break down cellular structures.

## Physics and Chemistry

**Entropy**: A thermodynamic quantity representing the unavailability of a system's thermal energy for conversion into mechanical work; often interpreted as a measure of disorder.

**Catalyst**: A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed in the process.

**Isotope**: Variants of a chemical element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

**Quantum Mechanics**: The branch of physics that describes the behaviour of particles at atomic and subatomic scales, where classical physics breaks down.

**Electromagnetic Spectrum**: The range of all types of electromagnetic radiation, ordered by frequency or wavelength (from radio waves through microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, to gamma rays).

## General Academic Terms

**Citation**: A reference to a source of information used in academic writing, allowing readers to locate the original work.

**Empirical**: Based on observation or experience rather than theory or pure logic.

**Qualitative Research**: Research that explores phenomena through non-numerical data (interviews, observations, texts) to understand meaning and context.

**Quantitative Research**: Research that collects numerical data and uses statistical analysis to test hypotheses.

**Longitudinal Study**: A research design in which data is collected from the same subjects repeatedly over a period of time.

**Meta-analysis**: A statistical procedure that combines the results of multiple scientific studies to derive conclusions about a body of research.

**Paradigm**: A framework containing the basic assumptions, ways of thinking, and methodology commonly accepted by members of a scientific community.

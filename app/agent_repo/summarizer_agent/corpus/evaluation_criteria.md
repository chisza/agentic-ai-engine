# Summary Evaluation Criteria

This document defines the scoring rubric used to evaluate the quality of a summary across four dimensions. Each dimension is scored from 1 to 10.

---

## 1. Completeness (1–10)

Measures how thoroughly the summary covers the key ideas in the source.

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10  | All major points, findings, and conclusions are present. No significant omissions. |
| 7–8   | Most key points covered; one minor detail missing. |
| 5–6   | Core argument present but one important supporting point or finding is absent. |
| 3–4   | Only partially covers the source; a major section or conclusion is missing. |
| 1–2   | Severely incomplete; misses most of the source content. |

**What to check**: Does the summary cover the main claim? The key evidence or method? The conclusion or implication? Any important limitations or caveats?

---

## 2. Conciseness (1–10)

Measures how efficiently the summary conveys information — absence of padding, redundancy, and irrelevant detail.

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10  | Every sentence carries new information. Length is appropriate for the source. |
| 7–8   | Mostly tight; one or two sentences could be cut without loss. |
| 5–6   | Noticeable redundancy or inclusion of peripheral detail. |
| 3–4   | Substantial padding; restates the same idea multiple times. |
| 1–2   | More filler than substance; the actual information could fit in half the words. |

**What to check**: Are any sentences repetitive? Does the summary include background the reader does not need? Is the length proportionate to the source (roughly 10–20% for long texts)?

---

## 3. Accuracy (1–10)

Measures factual faithfulness to the source — absence of errors, hallucinations, or distortions.

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10  | Every claim directly supported by the source. Uncertainty markers preserved. |
| 7–8   | One minor imprecision (e.g. slightly overstated finding) but no fabrications. |
| 5–6   | One factual error or a significant qualifier ("may", "in some cases") removed. |
| 3–4   | Multiple inaccuracies or one substantive fabrication. |
| 1–2   | Contradicts the source or introduces significant content not present in it. |

**What to check**: Do numbers, names, and dates match the source? Are causal claims ("X causes Y") supported or speculative? Are uncertainty markers ("suggests", "is associated with") preserved?

---

## 4. Clarity (1–10)

Measures how readable and understandable the summary is as a standalone document.

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9–10  | Flows naturally; any reader can follow it without the original. No jargon without explanation. |
| 7–8   | Mostly clear; one sentence is awkward or one term is unexplained. |
| 5–6   | Some sentences are hard to follow; unresolved pronouns or undefined abbreviations. |
| 3–4   | Frequently unclear; requires the original to interpret. |
| 1–2   | Incoherent or incomprehensible in isolation. |

**What to check**: Are all pronouns resolvable? Are abbreviations defined on first use? Are sentences grammatically correct? Does the summary read as a coherent text, not a list of disconnected fragments?

---

## Overall Score Interpretation

The four scores are independent; a summary can be complete but verbose, or concise but inaccurate. When reporting scores, always provide brief qualitative feedback per dimension so the author knows what to improve.

A total of 32–40 indicates a publication-ready summary.
A total of 24–31 indicates a good draft needing minor revision.
A total below 24 indicates significant issues that require rewriting.

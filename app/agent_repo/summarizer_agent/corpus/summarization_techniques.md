# Summarization Techniques and Best Practices

## What Makes a Good Summary

A high-quality summary preserves the essential meaning of the source while being significantly shorter. It should be readable as a standalone document — a reader who has not seen the original should come away with a clear, accurate understanding of the key points.

### Core Principles

**Completeness**: Cover all major ideas and arguments. Do not omit conclusions, key findings, or important caveats. A summary that misses a central claim is incomplete even if every sentence it does contain is accurate.

**Conciseness**: Remove redundancy, repetition, and peripheral detail. If two sentences in the source say the same thing differently, represent them with one sentence in the summary. Target 10–20% of the original length for long documents; shorter sources may need proportionally more.

**Accuracy**: Never introduce information not present in the source. Do not reinterpret, speculate, or add your own opinion. Preserve the original author's stance, including uncertainty markers ("may", "suggests", "is associated with").

**Clarity**: Use plain language. Resolve pronouns and abbreviations so the summary is self-contained. Prefer active voice and short sentences.

## Structural Approaches

### Extractive Summarization
Select the most informative sentences directly from the source. Best for highly technical or legal content where paraphrasing risks changing meaning. Weakness: can feel choppy; may not reflect the document's logical flow.

### Abstractive Summarization
Paraphrase and restructure ideas in new sentences. Produces more natural, readable summaries. Requires deep comprehension to avoid introducing errors. Best for narrative or argumentative texts.

### Hierarchical Summarization
For long documents: first summarize each section, then summarize the section summaries into a final overview. Preserves structure and is less likely to miss content buried in the middle of a long text.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Copying the introduction verbatim**: introductions are often broad; the actual contribution may be in the body.
- **Over-weighting the conclusion**: conclusions repeat what was already said; a summary of the conclusion alone misses the supporting evidence.
- **Hedging removal**: stripping qualifiers ("might", "in some cases") overstates findings.
- **Losing the "so what"**: always include why the content matters or what action/insight follows from it.
- **Length mismatch**: a 50-word summary of a 10,000-word technical report is almost certainly incomplete.

## Domain-Specific Guidance

### Scientific / Research Papers
Structure: problem → method → results → conclusion. Include sample sizes, key metrics, and limitations. Do not summarize the related work section unless it is the paper's main contribution.

### News Articles
Lead with the most newsworthy fact (who, what, when, where). Add context (why it matters) and any confirmed next steps. Omit quotes unless they are uniquely informative.

### Business Reports / Whitepapers
Lead with the recommendation or finding. Follow with the key evidence. Note any assumptions or scope limitations. Business readers scan for the bottom line first.

### Legal / Policy Documents
State the rule or decision first. Summarize the scope (who it applies to, when, under what conditions). Note exceptions and enforcement mechanisms.
